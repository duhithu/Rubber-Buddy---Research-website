Introduction

Natural rubber latex is utilized as a raw material for a wide range of products, ranging from domestic items to industrial machinery and shock absorbers. Therefore, natural rubber is regarded as an industrial raw material of strategic importance as well as one of the most adaptable agricultural products, having been used in about 50,000 products and industries worldwide.

The Amazonian rubber tree (Hevea brasiliensis), which is a native tree found in the Amazon rainforest in South America, is the major source of natural rubber latex. Being one of the recently domesticated plants, rubber plantations have flourished throughout the wet zone of Sri Lanka becoming one of the major export crops contributing over US$ 1092 million to Sri Lankan economy in 2021. However, rubber cultivators face several hardships in their daily lives. Pest control, identification and control of diseases, cover crops, and weed control can be regarded as some of the major issues amongst them.

Literature Survey

This study was conducted to identify the common pests, leaf diseases, cover crops, and weed species in rubber plantations and provide expert management strategies for the planters. Computer vision, image processing, and machine learning approaches can be seen as a common trend in recently conducted pest, weed, crop and leaf disease classification research.

Economically significant rubber diseases in Sri Lanka are classified into three categories: leaf, stem, and root diseases, of which the most common are the leaf diseases. Birdseye leaf disease, Colletotrichum leaf disease, Corynespora leaf fall disease, new leaf disease, Oidium and Pestalotiopsis are identified as leaf diseases with a significant impact on the rubber plantations. Early identification and application of cures and remedial measures for these diseases can ensure the longevity and sustainability of a rubber plantation.

Cover crops are beneficial for preventing soil erosion on rubber plantations. However, some cover crop species can promote the white root disease in middle-aged rubber plants. Therefore, proper identification of cover crop species is important in ensuring the health of the plantations.

Nutrient management of rubber plantations entails not only the proper application of fertilizers but also the management of weed species that invade the plantations. The weed species compete with the rubber plants for soil moisture, light, and nutrients. Some weeds can secrete chemical substances that inhibit the growth of rubber plants, while others attract harmful pests and diseases to the plantations. Due to the physical and biological characteristics of weeds that make them resilient, weed control has proven to be a struggle for the rubber planters. Therefore, it is critical to employ optimized weed control mechanisms capable of overcoming weed variety resilience.

Research Problem

Rubber production has a considerable impact on Sri Lanka's economy because it is one of the country's main export crops. Attacks from pests and numerous illnesses are the main causes of decreased rubber yields. If diseases and pests in the plants are not controlled, they may be severe enough to destroy the entire plantation. Invading weeds compete with the plants for nutrition, which slows down plant growth. Some weeds can release compounds that prevent rubber plants from growing, while others act as hosts for diseases and pests that are harmful.

To evaluate the nutrient states and identify disease infections in rubber plantations, specialists and researchers conduct a variety of laboratory experiments, such as foliar analyses. However, the majority of rubber planters are ignorant of these fields. When they suspect that diseases are attacking the plantation, they must thus consult an expert.

The primary goal of this research is to develop an application that provides expert solutions to common problems encountered by rubber cultivators in Sri Lanka. Through this study, we hope to provide support to the rubber planters who do not have the expert knowledge to solve their problems. The following are the research questions addressed through this study:

RQ1 - Are there any pest attacks on immature rubber plantations and rubber nurseries?

RQ2 – What control mechanisms are most appropriate to overcome these attacks?

RQ3 – Are there any leaf diseases in the rubber plantations?

RQ4 - How to treat the leaf disease in the plantations?

RQ5 – What are the cover crop varieties found in rubber plantations?

RQ6 – Can these cover crops increase the possibility of having root diseases in rubber plantations?

RQ7 – What are the weed varieties in the rubber plantations?

RQ8 – How to control the weed varieties in an optimized manner?

Research Objective

Main Objective

The main objective of this research was to develop a mobile application that will allow the rubber planters to apply expert solutions to their common agricultural problems. As mentioned in the preceding sections, the main issues that were addressed through this research are,

  1. Identification of pests in immature plantations and rubber nurseries and provision of control mechanisms.
  2. Identify rubber leaf diseases and provide preventive and remedial measures.
  3. Cover crop identification to determine whether they promote root diseases.
  4. Identify common weed varieties in rubber plantations and provide optimized weed control mechanisms.

Sub Objective

1. Identification of pests in immature plantations and rubber nurseries and provision of control mechanisms.

Even though mature rubber plantations are naturally immune to insect pest attacks due to the presence of latex, immature plantations and rubber nurseries suffer severe damage from them. Cockchafer grubs (Holotrichia serrata), mealybugs (Pseudococcidae spp.), red spider mites (Tetranychus urticae), scale insects (Coccoidea spp.) and yellow tea mites (Polyphagotarsonemus latus) are the major pests that attack the Sri Lankan rubber plantations. The goal of this component is to identify these pest species and provide the rubber planters with the expert knowledge required to control them.

2. Identification of rubber leaf diseases

There are numerous diseases that hinder the growth and development of rubber plants. Leaf diseases are one of the most prevalent types of diseases that affect rubber plants. Some of the most prominent leaf diseases that impact the Sri Lankan rubber plantations include Birds' Eye Spot Leaf Disease (Drechslera heveae), Colletotrichum Leaf Disease, Corynespora Leaf Fall Disease, "New" Leaf Disease, Oidium Leaf Disease, and Pestalotiopsis Leaf Disease. The purpose of the application is to detect rubber leaf diseases as early as possible, allowing planters to take preventive action to lessen the harm they cause.

3. Identification of common cover crop varieties cultivated and provide warnings on whether they promote root diseases

In rubber plantations, cover crops are grown as beneficial crops to reduce soil erosion and weed growth. However, one unintended negative effect of some cover crop species is the promotion of white root disease. The goal of this component is to determine whether the cover crop species grown in rubber plantations have the potential to spread root diseases.

4. Identify the common weed varieties and provide optimized control mechanisms.

Although fertilization is essential for maintaining the suitability of the land for a rubber plantation, weed and invasive species control is also required to ensure the rubber plants' continued growth. Despite the fact that the planters are capable of recognizing the majority of weed species, they lack knowledge on the optimal ways to control weeds. This is due to the many biological adaptations that weeds have developed over time to survive in hard or unfavorable environments. As a result, the developed application recognizes the weed species and proposes optimized weed management approaches.

Research Gap

Based on information extracted from the literature survey, there are several applications that support pest identification, disease identification, and weed control in cultivation lands. However, there aren't any apps that expressly target rubber plantations.

Leaf disease detection and in-depth disease descriptions are provided by the "Leaf Doctor" application. However, it does not provide any recommendations for treatments and is not accessible offline.

Without identifying the individual pests or diseases, "Plantix" and "Agrio" offer thorough explanations and treatment suggestions for diseases and pests in general. "Plant Disease Identifier" uses photos of plant leaves to identify issues and provide remedies and suggestions. However, all three of these applications cannot be used offline.

Both research studies, "Development of a Weed Identifier Mobile Application for Cambodian Rice Farmers" and "Weed Identifier" identify different weed types in cultivation lands. However, only the research "Weed Identifier" offers a comprehensive description of weeds. None of them have offline availability or weed control methods.

Rubber leaf diseases that are significant economically vary from nation to nation. There are no applications that specifically target the diseases of the rubber leaves that are prevalent in Sri Lanka. There are also no applications developed to identify cover crop species in Sri Lankan rubber plantations.

Methodology

This application was developed targeting the rubber plantation workers, plantation owners and rubber researchers as users. The four main services provided by the developed system are:

  1. Pest identification.
  2. Leaf disease identification.
  3. Cover crop identification.
  4. Weed identification.

  • Pest Identification

    The pest identification component focuses on the identification of common pests in immature rubber plantations and rubber nurseries. Under this component, 6 insect pests, namely, Cockchafer grubs (Holotrichia serrata), Cockchafers (Holotrichia serrata), Mealybugs (Pseudococcidae spp.), Red spider mites (Tetranychus urticae), Scale insects (Coccoidea spp.) and Yellow tea mites (Polyphagotarsonemus latus), were identified. This service uses a mobileNet version 2 CNN deep learning model to classify the pest images uploaded by the user and identify them. Then it provides a detailed description of the identified pest and appropriate pest control mechanisms for the planters.

  • Leaf Disease Identification

    The leaf disease identification component identifies 6 economically important leaf diseases; Birds’eye spot leaf disease (Drechslera heveae), Colletrotrichum leaf disease, Corynespora leaf fall disease, ‘New’ leaf disease, Oidium leaf disease, and Pestalotiopsis leaf disease. The uploaded images are classified by a CNN deep learning model and the diseases are detected. Then a detailed description of the identified disease and suitable treatments and recommendations are provided.

  • Cover Crop Identification

    In the cover crop identification component, the four most common cover crop species cultivated are identified. The cover crop species identified by this component are Centrosema pubescens, Desmodium ovalifolium, Pueraria phaseolodies, and Mucuna bracteate. This component also uses a deep learning CNN image classifier to identify the cover crop species and provide detailed descriptions of them. It also provides warnings on whether the identified cover crop species has the ability to promote root diseases in rubber plantations.

  • Weed Identification

    The weed identification component focuses on 10 common weed species found in rubber plantations, namely, Abiliya, Bowitiya, Gatakola, Girapala, Kakilla, Monarakudumbiya, Podisignomaran, Rilla, Thanampillu, and Wathupalu. The images uploaded by the user are identified by a CNN deep learning model and optimized weed control mechanisms are provided.

All four components take images as inputs to the system. The images can either be captured by the users on site in real time or be uploaded to the system through the device’s file system. The application is supported by a cloud database which stores the user data for commercialization purposes.
